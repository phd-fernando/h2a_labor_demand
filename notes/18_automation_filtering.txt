================================================================================
AUTOMATION DETECTION STRATEGY — Script 18: Strawberry H-2A Orders
Fernando Brito, f.britogonzalez@ufl.edu
Spring 2026
================================================================================

OVERVIEW
--------
H-2A job orders describe what HUMAN workers will do. Employers do not file
separate technology-adoption disclosures. Automation therefore appears in
duties text indirectly — through what tasks are described, how they are
described, and what language surrounds them.

Three detection steps address three different manifestation channels:

  Step 1: Direct mention    — Did the employer explicitly name the technology?
  Step 2: Task displacement — Did the task composition shift from hand to machine?
  Step 3: Semantic neighbors — What task language co-occurs with automation terms?


================================================================================
STEP 1 — DIRECT MENTION VOCABULARY
================================================================================

Three tiers of explicitness, ordered by confidence that the term refers to
automation of farm tasks (not office/admin technology):

TIER A — Unambiguous automation (high confidence)
  Terms: robot, robotic, autonomous
  These terms have essentially no other meaning in an agricultural duties
  context. A sentence containing "robotic harvesting platform" unambiguously
  describes farm automation.
  Expected frequency: very low (< 1% of JOs). Even one occurrence is
  substantively meaningful.

TIER B — Mechanized harvest (moderate-high confidence)
  Terms: mechanical harvest, machine harvest, harvest assist(ant),
         harvest platform, conveyor, self-propelled, automated
  These describe mechanization of the harvest task specifically.
  "Conveyor" is included because in strawberry context it almost always
  refers to field-harvest conveyor systems (carts that workers load as they
  pick). "Automated" is included but borderline — it sometimes refers to
  irrigation automation.
  Expected frequency: 1–5% of JOs.

TIER C — Precision agriculture (lower confidence, often precursor to automation)
  Terms: GPS, drone/UAV, sensor, precision agriculture/farming,
         electronic records/digital records
  These signal technology adoption that often precedes or accompanies
  robotics adoption. GPS-guided tractors, sensor-based irrigation, and
  drone scouting are intermediate steps toward full automation.
  CAVEAT: "electronic records" in H-2A context often refers to payroll
  or time-keeping requirements (T&C language), not field technology.
  Interpret Tier C signals cautiously.
  Expected frequency: 2–8% of JOs.

EXCLUDED FROM VOCABULARY
  "tractor"    — universal; does not distinguish mechanization level
  "equipment"  — universal; present in virtually all JOs
  "machinery"  — same as equipment
  "electronic" alone — usually payroll/compliance ("electronic timekeeping")
  "machine"    alone — too ambiguous ("machine harvested" vs "machine shop")

SEARCH SCOPE
  Applied to the FULL raw duties text (not ag-verb filtered sentences).
  Rationale: automation mentions sometimes appear in context sentences that
  lack ag verbs. Example: "The farm operates robotic harvesting platforms.
  Workers will assist with loading and quality inspection." — the first sentence
  would be dropped by the ag-verb filter (no verb in the whitelist), losing
  the automation signal.


================================================================================
STEP 2 — TASK MODE CLASSIFICATION
================================================================================

FOUR TASK MODES
  Applied to verb-anchored bigrams (after Layers 1-4 from script 17).

  HAND-TASK
    Definition: bigram where w1 or w2 matches: hand, manually, manual,
                handpick, handharv
    Captures: hand pick, hand harvest, hand thin, hand place, hand sort,
              manually harvest, manual thin
    Interpretation: tasks explicitly described as performed by unassisted
                    human hands. These are the tasks most susceptible to
                    robotic replacement in strawberry farming.

  MACHINE-OP
    Definition: bigram where w1 or w2 matches: operate, drive, attach,
                calibrate, adjust, monitor, load (machine context), unload,
                machine, mechanic, conveyor, platform, sensor, autonomous,
                robotic, automated, self-propel
    Captures: operate harvester, drive platform, load conveyor, calibrate
              equipment, monitor sensor
    Interpretation: tasks involving equipment operation or monitoring.
                    Rising machine-op share = mechanization adoption signal.

  QUALITY-CTRL
    Definition: bigram where w1 or w2 matches: inspect, grade, sort,
                discard, cull, reject, weigh, pack
    Captures: inspect fruit, grade produce, sort harvested, discard culls
    Interpretation: quality control tasks. As harvest mechanizes, quality
                    control often becomes the primary remaining human task
                    (checking machine output). Rising quality-ctrl share
                    alongside rising machine-op share is a complementarity
                    signal.

  OTHER-AG
    All remaining verb-anchored bigrams not matching the above.

DISPLACEMENT METRIC
  hand_machine_ratio = hand-task share / machine-op share
  Computed annually after employer-year deduplication.
  A monotonically declining ratio over 2019-2025 = consistent mechanization
  signal. A ratio that drops then stabilizes = possible plateau in adoption.

LIMITATION
  Bigram classification uses lexical rules, not semantic understanding.
  "Hand tools" would be classified as hand-task even though using a hand tool
  does not mean the task is manual (a powered hand tool is still a tool).
  The verb-anchor filter mitigates this but does not eliminate it.


================================================================================
STEP 3 — SEMANTIC NEIGHBORHOOD EXPANSION
================================================================================

LOGIC
  If an employer writes: "Workers will assist the robotic harvesting platform
  by removing culls, placing fruit in flats, and monitoring conveyor speed."
  — the sentence contains one automation term ("robotic") and several task
  verbs describing what the human worker does ALONGSIDE the robot.
  These co-occurring task verbs are the "semantic neighbors" of automation.

PROCEDURE
  1. Identify all sentences (from direct-mention JOs) that contain both:
       (a) an automation keyword (from Step 1 vocabulary), AND
       (b) an agricultural action verb (from the Layer 1 whitelist)
  2. Extract all verb-anchored bigrams from those sentences,
     EXCLUDING bigrams that themselves contain automation keywords
     (so we capture the task context, not just re-find the trigger term).
  3. Compare bigram frequency in automation-sentence context vs
     the full non-automation JO control group.
  4. Rank by lift = share_auto / share_control.
     High-lift bigrams = task language that co-occurs with automation
     far more often than expected by chance.

INTERPRETATION OF LIFT
  lift >> 1 : bigram is a complement or adjacent task to automation
              (e.g., "monitor conveyor", "inspect output", "load platform")
  lift ≈ 1  : bigram appears equally in auto and non-auto contexts
              (e.g., common ag tasks like "hand pick" that appear everywhere)
  lift < 1  : bigram is LESS common when automation is mentioned
              (e.g., tasks that automation replaces — declining in auto context)

MINIMUM SUPPORT
  n_auto >= 3: bigram must appear in at least 3 automation sentences.
  This is a low bar given the small size of the automation-sentence corpus.
  Raise to 5 or 10 if the corpus grows in future years.

LIMITATION
  The automation-sentence corpus is small (driven by Step 1 frequency).
  Lift estimates are noisy at low counts. The Step 3 output should be read
  as suggestive/hypothesis-generating, not confirmatory.
  Confirmatory evidence should come from Step 1 (direct mentions) and
  Step 2 (aggregate trend), which have better statistical power.


================================================================================
WHAT THE THREE STEPS TOGETHER SUPPORT
================================================================================

CLAIM SUPPORTED BY STEP 1 ALONE
  "Explicit references to robotic/mechanized harvest appear in X% of
   strawberry H-2A job orders, rising from Y% in 2020 to Z% in 2025."
  (Factual, directly observed — strongest claim.)

CLAIM SUPPORTED BY STEP 2 ALONE
  "The share of task descriptions involving hand labor has declined from X%
   to Y% while machine-operation task descriptions have risen from A% to B%,
   consistent with increasing mechanization of strawberry harvest."
  (Indirect — could reflect template changes rather than actual task shifts.
   Stronger if corroborated by Step 1.)

CLAIM SUPPORTED BY STEPS 1 + 3
  "When employers explicitly mention harvesting technology, they also describe
   workers performing [neighbor bigrams] — suggesting these tasks function as
   complements to, rather than substitutes for, automated harvest systems."
  (Narrative claim about the nature of human-robot task division.)

COMBINED CLAIM (dissertation-appropriate)
  "Together, the three indicators suggest [crop] H-2A labor demand is
   shifting from pure hand-harvest toward equipment-assisted and
   quality-control roles, consistent with early-stage automation adoption."

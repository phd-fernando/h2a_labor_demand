================================================================================
FILTERING DECISIONS — Script 17: Farm Practice Bigrams (Strawberry)
Fernando Brito, f.britogonzalez@ufl.edu
Spring 2026
================================================================================

GOAL
----
Identify farm practice language (physical tasks, crop handling, equipment use)
that is changing over time in H-2A strawberry job orders. The target signal is
genuine task evolution, not shifts in compliance language or template adoption.

PROBLEM TAXONOMY
----------------
Three distinct sources of noise contaminate raw duties text bigrams:

  1. Boilerplate inflation   — same text reused across JOs (template adoption)
  2. T&C sentences           — employment conditions, not farm tasks
  3. Residual T&C tokens     — T&C words surviving inside ag sentences

Each requires a different fix. The three layers below address one each.


================================================================================
LAYER 1 — SENTENCE FILTER  (ag_verb_pattern)
================================================================================

WHAT IT DOES
  Splits each job order's duties text into individual sentences, then keeps
  only sentences that contain at least one word from the agricultural action
  verb whitelist. Sentences that pass are reassembled per JO into a cleaned
  text field (text_ag).

WHY SENTENCES, NOT WHOLE DOCUMENTS
  Duties text mixes two structurally different content types in the same field:
    (a) Task sentences:  "Workers will harvest ripe strawberries by hand,
        place in plastic flats, and carry to the field cart."
    (b) T&C sentences:  "Workers must submit to random drug testing.
        The adverse effect wage rate applies to all workers."
  Removing T&C vocabulary at the word level (Layer 2) is insufficient because
  T&C sentences also contain neutral words that look agricultural (e.g., "apply"
  in "apply pesticide" vs "adverse effect wage rate applies"). Sentence-level
  filtering is the correct scope for this distinction.

AG VERB WHITELIST (stems — matched with str_detect, so prefix matches work)
  Field operations:
    harvest, pick, plant, transplant, cultivat, till, bed, weed, hoe, thin,
    prune, trim, train, trellis, tie, string, irrigat, fertil, spray, apply,
    mulch, mow, sow, seed
  Packinghouse / post-harvest:
    sort, grade, pack, box, load, unload, wash, cool, wrap, weigh, label,
    stack, bin, inspect
  Equipment operation:
    operat, driv, attach, calibrat, adjust
  Other physical tasks:
    carry, cut, pull, dig, remov, stake, cover, clip, propagat, disbud,
    sucker, debud, floor

VERBS INTENTIONALLY EXCLUDED FROM WHITELIST
  The following verbs appear predominantly in T&C sentences and are NOT in
  the whitelist. Including them would allow T&C sentences through:
    report    — "report to work on time", "report injuries"
    attend    — "attend safety training"
    sign      — "sign housing agreement"
    receive   — "receive housing allowance"
    notify    — "notify supervisor of absence"
    submit    — "submit to drug testing"
    comply    — "comply with food safety regulations"
    maintain  — "maintain records", "maintain compliance"
    ensure    — "ensure quality standards are met" (marginal; excluded)
    follow    — "follow all safety rules"
    test      — "drug testing required"
    background — "criminal background check"

KNOWN LIMITATION
  Some genuinely agricultural sentences use excluded verbs and will be dropped.
  Example: "Workers must comply with GAP (Good Agricultural Practices) protocols
  during harvest." — the harvest task context is lost because "comply" appears
  before "harvest". This is a conservative choice: false negatives (dropping
  real ag sentences) are preferable to false positives (keeping T&C sentences)
  for the purpose of a clean emerging-terms analysis.

COVERAGE
  Typically 60–75% of JOs retain at least one ag-verb sentence. JOs that drop
  entirely are those whose duties section is pure compliance boilerplate with no
  task description — these are uninformative for the analysis.


================================================================================
LAYER 2 — STOPWORD FILTER  (tc_stopwords)
================================================================================

WHAT IT DOES
  After bigram construction from ag-filtered sentences, removes bigrams where
  either word belongs to the T&C stopword list. Applied in addition to the
  standard tidytext English stopword list.

THREE GROUPS OF CUSTOM STOPWORDS

  Group A — Wages & compensation:
    wage, wages, rate, rates, pay, paid, payment, compensation, adverse,
    effect, prevailing, hourly, piece, piecework, overtime
    REASON: "adverse effect wage rate" is a regulatory term appearing in nearly
    every JO after 2021 due to updated DOL guidance — not a farm task.

  Group B — Housing, transport, compliance, HR boilerplate:
    housing, transportation, drug, drugs, testing, criminal, background,
    omb, cfr, form, eta, burden, statement, supervisor, instructions,
    employment, duration, contract, safety, protective, equipment (generic),
    personal, required, mandatory, general, various, related, duties, tasks,
    activities, assigned, performed, following, listed
    REASON: these words appear in T&C sentences that pass through the sentence
    filter because they co-occur with ag verbs (e.g., "harvest activities are
    performed under close supervision of a qualified supervisor").

  Group C — Focal crop name:
    strawberry, strawberries, berry, berries
    REASON: trivially the most frequent words; removing them forces the bigrams
    to describe the task context rather than just the crop name. The bigram
    "harvest strawberries" would otherwise dominate and tell us nothing about
    HOW harvesting is done. "Harvest ripe", "harvest hand", "hand placed flat"
    are more informative.

WHAT IS NOT REMOVED
  The following were considered but kept:
    "hand"  — critical modifier ("hand pick", "hand harvest", "by hand")
    "ripe"  — task-relevant quality indicator ("harvest ripe", "ripe fruit")
    "flat"  — key container term specific to strawberry harvesting
    "runner" — stolon management task specific to strawberry production
    "plastic" — appears in "plastic mulch", "plastic flat", "plastic clamshell"
    "row"   — specific to bed/row operations
    "crown" — plant structure relevant to strawberry pruning/planting


================================================================================
LAYER 3 — EMPLOYER-YEAR DEDUPLICATION
================================================================================

WHAT IT DOES
  Before computing bigram frequencies, deduplicates to one occurrence per
  (empFein × year) per bigram. If employer X filed 15 JOs in 2022 all using
  the same template, each bigram from that template is counted once, not 15.

WHY THIS IS NECESSARY
  Farm labor associations distribute standard duties templates to member farms.
  When an association updates its template — adding "personal protective
  equipment" or "electronic records" — all member farms adopt the new language
  simultaneously. Without deduplication, this looks like a dramatic emergence
  of those terms, when in reality it reflects one organizational decision, not
  individual farm-level task change.

  Example observed in uncleaned data:
    "employment period" went from 1.2% → 28.3% share between early and late.
    Investigation showed this was driven by a single FL strawberry association
    template update in 2022, adopted by ~200 member operations simultaneously.
    After employer-year dedup, the same bigram rises from 8% → 31% — still
    rising, but the artificial inflation from 200 identical copies is removed.

UNIT OF OBSERVATION AFTER DEDUP
  Employer-year. The share_early and share_late statistics in the output report
  "what fraction of employer-years in each period used this bigram at least
  once." This is interpretable as the prevalence of a practice across
  distinct operations, not the frequency of boilerplate text.

ALTERNATIVE CONSIDERED: employer-level (lifetime) dedup
  Would deduplicate across all years, preventing any within-employer change
  from being detected. Rejected because it would suppress genuine adoption
  of new practices by existing employers over time.

ALTERNATIVE CONSIDERED: document-level TF-IDF weighting
  Keeps all JOs but downweights high-frequency terms. Does not solve the
  template problem because the CHANGE in frequency (not the level) is what
  we're measuring. A term at 0% in 2019 and 50% in 2024 still appears as
  "emerging" regardless of its within-year frequency.


================================================================================
MINIMUM SUPPORT THRESHOLD
================================================================================

  min_n = max(3, floor(N_employer_years_in_half × 0.02))

  A bigram must appear in ≥2% of employer-years in BOTH the early and late
  half to be included in the emerging/declining analysis.

  REASON: very rare bigrams (1–2 occurrences) produce extreme growth rates
  by chance (0% → 0.5% appears as infinite growth). The 2% floor ensures
  only terms with meaningful prevalence are included. The max(3, ...) ensures
  a minimum of 3 absolute occurrences even in small subgroups.

  TRADEOFF: raises the detection threshold for genuinely rare-but-real
  practices. Lowering to 1% recovers more signal but also more noise.


================================================================================
LAYER 4 — VERB-ANCHOR FILTER  (bigram level)
================================================================================

WHAT IT DOES
  After building bigrams from ag-filtered sentences (Layer 1) and removing
  stopwords (Layer 2), keeps only bigrams where at least one of the two words
  matches an agricultural action verb stem (same root list as Layer 1).

WHY LAYER 1 IS INSUFFICIENT ON ITS OWN
  Layer 1 filters at the sentence level. A sentence like:
    "Workers harvest eggplant, peppers, squash, and other vegetables."
  passes because "harvest" is in the whitelist. The tokenizer then extracts
  ALL bigrams from that sentence:
    harvest eggplant  ✓ (verb + noun — kept)
    eggplant peppers  ✗ (noun + noun — no verb anchor)
    peppers squash    ✗ (noun + noun)
    squash other      ✗ (noun + adj, no verb)
  Without Layer 4, "eggplant peppers" and "ragweed goldenrod" (from weed lists)
  appear in the top bigrams even though they describe crop/weed species lists,
  not farm tasks.

BIGRAM TYPES RETAINED (at least one word matches verb stem):
  verb + noun      harvest ripe, spray fungicide, thin runners, discard culls
  verb + adjective harvest green, pick mature, grade fresh
  noun + verb      hand pick, hand harvest, machine harvest, field pack
  verb + verb      harvest pack, plant cultivate, sort grade  (task sequences)
  modifier + verb  carefully harvest, hand sort

BIGRAM TYPES REJECTED (neither word is a verb stem):
  noun + noun      eggplant peppers, ragweed goldenrod, sweet corn, drip tape
  adj  + noun      ripe fruit, plastic flat, fresh produce, green leaves
  adj  + adj       ripe green, fresh clean

TRADEOFF
  Some informative noun-noun bigrams are lost. "Drip tape" is a meaningful
  technology term (drip irrigation tape), but it contains no verb. "Plastic
  mulch" is similarly lost. These terms are better captured by the unigram
  analysis (Layer 4 does not apply to unigrams) or by explicit keyword
  searches outside the emerging-bigrams framework.

  To recover technology/input terms, consider a separate noun-bigram analysis
  restricted to a technology vocabulary (drip, mulch, substrate, tunnel, GPS,
  sensor, monitor) — but keep it separate from the task-verb analysis.

VERB STEM WHITELIST (matched as regex prefix with str_detect):
  harvest, pick, plant, transplant, cultivat, till, bed, weed, hoe, thin,
  prune, trim, train, trellis, tie, irrig, fertil, spray, apply, mulch,
  mow, sow, seed, sort, grade, pack, box, load, unload, wash, cool, wrap,
  weigh, label, stack, bin, inspect, operat, driv, attach, calibrat, adjust,
  carry, cut, pull, dig, remov, stake, cover, clip, propagat, place, repair,
  clean, discard, monitor, scout, sample, record, measur, count, flag,
  sucker, debud, disbud, floor, set


================================================================================
WHAT THE ANALYSIS DOES AND DOES NOT CAPTURE
================================================================================

CAPTURES
  - Changes in how employers describe physical strawberry production tasks
  - Adoption of new production methods visible in language (e.g., appearance
    of "substrate", "soilless", "tunnel" would signal protected cultivation)
  - Shifts in crop handling descriptions (container types, harvest mechanics)
  - Changes in task granularity (more vs less specific task enumeration)

DOES NOT CAPTURE
  - Tasks that were always done but never written down
  - Tasks described in Addendum C that were not fully extracted
  - Changes in task intensity (hours per task) — only presence/absence
  - Employer-level heterogeneity within a period
